Let's revisit some of the main milestones of deep learning. First, there was a boom in the use of convolutional neural networks for modelling complex relationships, especially in the case of visual stimuli and the field of computer vision. Recurrent neural networks (RNNs) also showed promising results for natural language processing. These networks were widely used to process sequential data such as sentences or time-series data, thanks to their ability to memorise relevant features from previous input data. A recurrent network is characterised by a notion of memory in its processing. However, the sequential nature of recurrent networks creates challenges in terms of computational cost and the ability to perform parallel training across many machines simultaneously.

Motivated by this, in 2017 a team at Google Brain led by Vaswani and his collaborators published a very famous paper called "Attention Is All You Need". This paper introduced the Transformer architecture for the first time. We will now look at the architecture proposed by this team.

Transformers consist of two main components: first the encoder, and then the decoder. Initially, the model receives an input sequence — for example, the phrase "I am a student". This sentence is encoded in a representation space. Thanks to our model, a single representation is found for the sentence as a whole, and in addition the encoder will find a representation for each of the words that make up that sentence.

On the other hand, the decoder takes these features we just described and establishes the relevance of each of them in order to arrive at a new prediction. For example, in this case it could be a Transformer for the translation task, and the output would be that same sentence "I am a student" but in another language. We would have a decoder to translate the sentence into French, for example, and another to translate it into English. The encoder, then, extracts the most relevant features, and the decoder — based on those features — produces the expected final prediction.

Before passing through the encoder, the data must be pre-processed in the following way. For example, if the input phrase is "I am a student", the first thing we do is tokenise it — that is, divide it into smaller units. For example, we can convert it into a vector where each word is a unit. Then each of these elements is assigned a unique integer value: n1, n2, n3. Each of the elements of this vector is called a token. We can think of tokenisation as a dictionary that maps words to numbers.

In the Input Embedding block, the model represents each token in an alternative vector space — potentially of higher dimension — where the semantic information of the words is captured. For example, words with similar meanings should be located near each other in this representation space, while words with different meanings should be farther apart, as illustrated in this figure, where we can see a two-dimensional representation of the words.

The Positional Encoding block adds information to each token to give it context about its position relative to the other tokens. This enables the model to understand the order in which the tokens appear in the text.

Now that we have represented the words in a space where we encode their semantic and positional relationships in the text, we pass this information to the encoder block.

Let's now talk more about the encoder. The encoder is composed of multiple layers with self-attention mechanisms, feed-forward networks, normalisation layers, and residual connections. What truly makes Transformers unique is the self-attention mechanism, since it allows the encoder to weigh the relative importance of each element in the input sequence with respect to the other elements. In this way, the encoder can focus on the information that is most relevant and capture long-range dependencies between the data very effectively. The feed-forward layer, on the other hand, adds a non-linearity to each layer of the encoder with respect to the self-attention mechanism. The function of the encoder, then, is to create a single representation for the entire input sequence, as well as a representation for each of the elements of that sequence.

Let's now talk about the decoder. The decoder is also composed of multiple layers containing self-attention mechanisms and feed-forward networks — multilayer perceptrons or even single-layer ones. However, the decoder layer additionally includes what we call a cross-attention mechanism, which relates the encoder's features to the predictions the decoder has previously made. Finally, the last layer of the decoder passes through a linear network that projects the features and generates a final prediction.

In conclusion, Transformers are a very recent neural network architecture, and they are composed of an encoder and a decoder. The encoder extracts the most relevant features of the input data thanks to a self-attention mechanism in all of its layers. The decoder takes the previous predictions together with the encoder's features in order to produce a new prediction. In addition, Transformers can be trained in parallel and have the ability to be pre-trained on a large dataset, which has a great impact in practice.
