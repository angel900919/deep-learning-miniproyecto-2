Let's first define pre-training and discuss its importance. This is a crucial stage in the development of artificial intelligence models that, like Transformers, are trained on large amounts of data without any specific task as an objective. During pre-training, the model learns to capture complex patterns and structures present in the input data, which allows it to acquire a broad general understanding of the domain and to learn extremely rich representations of the data.

Pre-training is carried out on large datasets — such as text corpora from the web, books, articles, or any other available textual resource. By training the model on a diverse and extensive corpus, it is expected to acquire deep knowledge of the language and to be able to capture the essential characteristics of different words and linguistic constructions.

In the case of Transformers for natural language processing — the native domain in which they were created — pre-training involves exposing the model to a large amount of text without any kind of label. The goal is for the model to acquire a deep understanding of the language, including key features such as semantics, syntax, and the contextual relationships between words and phrases.

During the pre-training process, the Transformer-based model is fed with text sequences — such as sentences or text fragments. The model learns to predict the next word in a sequence based on the previous context. This is achieved through the use of a self-attention architecture, which allows the model to assign different weights or levels of importance to the input words based on their contextual relevance, in order to predict the last word of the text fragment.

After the pre-training stage, where the model has learned general representations of the language, we proceed with transfer learning, or fine-tuning. This stage involves training the already pre-trained model for a specific task or particular domain, generally using a much smaller and usually labelled dataset. This last stage is typically supervised.

Fine-tuning is essential for adapting a pre-trained model to much more specific tasks in natural language processing. Examples of these tasks are text classification, sentiment analysis, translation, text generation, among others. Through fine-tuning, the model specialises in the desired task and adjusts its weights to obtain better performance on that specific task.

Fine-tuning allows the model to take advantage of the general knowledge it acquired during pre-training and now adapt it to the specific task or domain. This is especially beneficial when the available training datasets — although labelled — are much smaller and more limited, since the pre-trained model has already captured a large part of the linguistic and contextual structure and can now apply it to this more restricted domain.

In conclusion, pre-training of Transformers involves training the model on large amounts of unlabelled data. Thanks to this methodology, the Transformer learns general representations of the language and captures very complex patterns and structures. This provides a solid foundation for subsequently performing transfer learning and adapting the model to specific natural language processing tasks. Fine-tuning, or transfer learning, is this process of adjusting and specialising a pre-trained Transformer model for a specific task. Fine-tuning makes it possible to adapt the model to more specific NLP tasks and thus improve its performance in particular domains, taking advantage of the knowledge it has previously acquired during pre-training.
